<Callout>
Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID).
Reduces pain, fever, and inflammation by blocking prostaglandin production.
</Callout>

---

## Indication

- Arthritis (rheumatoid, osteoarthritis)
- Muscle or bone pain
- Acute gout
- Menstrual pain
- Post-surgery pain

### Children

- Usually not used under 12 years.
- For juvenile arthritis: 1–3 mg/kg/day in divided doses (specialist advice only).

---

## Administration

- Take with or after food.
- Swallow tablets whole (do not crush).
- Deep IM injection into gluteal muscle only.
- Avoid using oral and topical forms together.

---

## Contraindications

- Allergy to diclofenac, aspirin, or other NSAIDs
- Asthma or rash after NSAID use
- History of stomach ulcer or bleeding
- Severe heart, liver, or kidney disease
- Third trimester of pregnancy

---

## Adverse Reactions

- **Common**

  - Stomach upset, nausea, diarrhoea
  - Headache, dizziness
  - Rash or itching

- **Serious (rare)**

  - Stomach bleeding or ulcer
  - Liver or kidney problems
  - Heart attack or stroke (long-term use)
  - Severe allergic reaction or asthma attack

---

## Pregnancy and Lactation

<Callout>
**Pregnancy:** Avoid in 3rd trimester.
Use only if clearly needed in early pregnancy, at the lowest effective dose.

**Lactation:** Passes into milk in small amounts; short-term use is usually safe.
Monitor baby for stomach upset.
</Callout>
