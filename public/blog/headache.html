Headache: A Comprehensive Guide

Understanding Types, Pathophysiology, Diagnosis, and Management of Headaches.

Definition and Epidemiology

Headache is defined as pain in any region of the head and is a very common neurological symptom. Headaches are categorized into primary (not caused by an underlying condition) and secondary (symptoms of another disorder) types, with diagnostic criteria provided by the International Classification of Headache Disorders, 3rd edition (ICHD-3).

Pathophysiology

Migraine

  • Altered neuronal excitability
  • Trigeminal nervous system activation
  • Cortical Spreading Depression (CSD)
  • Neurogenic inflammation
  • Central sensitization

Tension-Type Headache (TTH)

  • Peripheral mechanisms (episodic)
  • Central sensitization (chronic)
  • Dysregulation of descending pain pathways

Cluster Headache

  • Hypothalamic dysfunction
  • Trigeminal autonomic reflex
  • CGRP involvement
  • Autonomic symptoms

Clinical Presentation

Migraine

  • 4-72 hours duration
  • Unilateral, pulsating, moderate-to-severe
  • Aggravated by activity
  • Nausea/vomiting, photophobia/phonophobia
  • May have aura

Tension-Type Headache (TTH)

  • 30 mins to 7 days duration
  • Bilateral, pressing/tightening, mild-to-moderate
  • Not aggravated by activity
  • No nausea/vomiting; +/- photophobia or phonophobia

Cluster Headache

  • 15-180 mins per attack, in bouts
  • Severe unilateral orbital/supraorbital/temporal pain
  • Ipsilateral autonomic symptoms (lacrimation, rhinorrhea, etc.)
  • Restlessness/agitation

All criteria based on ICHD-3.

Diagnostic Criteria & Red Flags

Diagnosis of primary headaches relies on detailed history and neurological examination, following ICHD-3 criteria. A normal exam is crucial to rule out secondary causes. The SNOOP10 mnemonic highlights red flags for secondary headaches requiring investigation:

Key Diagnostic Steps

  • Detailed History (Onset, Duration, Frequency, Quality, Intensity, Location, Triggers, etc.)
  • Neurological Examination

Red Flags (SNOOP10)

  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, cancer)
  • Neurologic symptoms/abnormal signs (altered mental status, focal deficits)
  • Onset: New onset or change in pattern (sudden/thunderclap, >50 yrs)
  • Other associated conditions (trauma, drug use, pregnancy)
  • Progressive headache pattern
  • Papilledema
  • Postural aggravation
  • Precipitated by Valsalva/Exertion
  • Pregnancy

Management Overview

Management strategies vary by headache type, including acute (abortive) and preventive therapies. Accurate diagnosis is key to effective treatment.

Migraine Management

Acute Treatment

Aims for rapid and consistent attack treatment.

Mild-to-Moderate Attacks:
  • NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen)
  • Acetaminophen
  • Combination Analgesics (Acetaminophen/Aspirin/Caffeine)
Moderate-to-Severe Attacks:
  • Triptans (Sumatriptan, Rizatriptan)
  • CGRP Receptor Antagonists (Gepants: Rimegepant, Ubrogepant)
  • Ditans (Lasmiditan)
  • Antiemetics (Metoclopramide)

Note: Specific dosages and contraindications apply.

Preventive Treatment

Considered for frequent headaches or significant disability.

Oral Agents:
  • Beta-blockers (Propranolol)
  • Anticonvulsants (Topiramate)
  • Antidepressants (Amitriptyline)
Other Therapies:
  • CGRP Monoclonal Antibodies (mAbs)
  • OnabotulinumtoxinA (Botox for chronic migraine)
  • Neuromodulation Devices

Tension-Type Headache (TTH) Management

Acute Treatment

  • Simple Analgesics (Aspirin, Ibuprofen, Acetaminophen)
  • Combination Analgesics

Use sparingly to avoid medication overuse headache.

Preventive Treatment

  • Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs: Amitriptyline)
  • Non-pharmacological approaches (Biofeedback, CBT)

Cluster Headache Management

Acute Treatment

  • 100% Oxygen (high flow)
  • Triptans (Sumatriptan SC injection, Zolmitriptan nasal spray)

Oral triptans are generally too slow.

Preventive Treatment

  • First-line: Verapamil
  • Second-line/Add-on: Lithium, Topiramate
  • Galcanezumab (episodic cluster headache)

Special Populations

Pregnancy and Lactation

  • Acute Migraine: Acetaminophen first-line. NSAIDs (first two trimesters). Triptans (low-risk if needed).
  • Preventive Migraine: Beta-blockers, Amitriptyline preferred. Valproate/Topiramate generally contraindicated.

Children and Adolescents

  • Acute Migraine: Ibuprofen, Acetaminophen first-line. Triptans approved for adolescents.
  • Preventive Migraine: Non-pharmacological emphasized. Topiramate/Propranolol for refractory cases.

Controversies & Recent Updates

Recent Updates

  • CGRP Pathway Therapies: mAbs (Erenumab, Fremanezumab, etc.) and oral gepants (Rimegepant, Ubrogepant) revolutionizing migraine prevention and acute treatment.
  • Ditans (Lasmiditan): Non-vasoconstrictive acute treatment option.
  • Neuromodulation Devices: Growing evidence for nVNS, sTMS.

Controversies

  • Medication Overuse Headache (MOH): Frequent acute medication use can worsen headaches; withdrawal strategies debated.
  • Refractory Migraine: Optimizing sequencing of newer therapies for non-responders.
  • Subtype Classification: Continued research into biomarkers for individualized treatment.
