Throat Infections in Children

Understanding Pharyngitis: Causes, Symptoms, Diagnosis, and Management

Definition and Epidemiology

Throat infections, also known as pharyngitis or tonsillopharyngitis, are acute inflammations of the pharynx and/or tonsils. In children, these are primarily viral, accounting for 70-85% of cases. The most common bacterial cause is Group A Streptococcus (GAS), specifically Streptococcus pyogenes, responsible for 15-30% of cases in children and adolescents, with a peak incidence between 5 and 15 years of age. Less common bacterial agents include Arcanobacterium haemolyticum, Mycoplasma pneumoniae, and Chlamydophila pneumoniae. Fungal infections are rare and typically affect immunocompromised individuals. Non-infectious causes like allergies, irritants, or gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) can mimic these symptoms [1, 2, 3].

Pathophysiology

The underlying mechanism involves pathogen entry and replication in the pharyngeal mucosa, triggering a localized inflammatory response.

Viral Pharyngitis

  • Viruses such as rhinovirus, adenovirus, influenza virus, parainfluenza virus, enterovirus, respiratory syncytial virus, and Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infect pharyngeal epithelial cells.
  • Viral replication leads to cell damage and an inflammatory cascade involving cytokine release, causing vasodilation, edema, and pain.
  • EBV can cause infectious mononucleosis, characterized by significant lymphoid hyperplasia in the tonsils and cervical lymph nodes [4, 5].

Bacterial Pharyngitis (Group A Streptococcus)

  • Streptococcus pyogenes attaches to host epithelial cells via surface proteins like M protein, lipoteichoic acid, and F protein.
  • It produces virulence factors including streptolysins O and S, pyrogenic exotoxins, and streptokinase.
  • These factors help evade host defenses and cause tissue damage leading to exudative pharyngitis.
  • The M protein is linked to post-streptococcal sequelae such as acute rheumatic fever and post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis [6, 7].

Clinical Presentation

Symptoms vary by etiology, with considerable overlap between viral and bacterial infections.

General Symptoms

Include sore throat (odynophagia), difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), fever, headache, and malaise.

Viral Pharyngitis

Often accompanied by coryza, cough, hoarseness, conjunctivitis, oral ulcers, or diarrhea. Adenovirus can cause pharyngoconjunctival fever [4, 8].

Bacterial Pharyngitis (Group A Streptococcus)

Abrupt onset of sore throat, painful swallowing, fever, headache, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, tonsillar exudates, palatal petechiae, tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, and absence of cough.

Scarlet Fever

Fine sandpaper-like rash, strawberry tongue, and desquamation during recovery [1, 9].

Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)

Severe unilateral sore throat, trismus, muffled voice, drooling, and uvular deviation [10].

Diagnostic Criteria

Goal is to distinguish GAS from viral causes to avoid unnecessary antibiotics.

  • Modified McIsaac Score
  • RADT testing
  • Throat culture (gold standard)
  • Molecular NAAT assays

Management

Symptomatic treatment for viral causes. Antibiotics for confirmed GAS.

Viral Pharyngitis

  • Acetaminophen or ibuprofen
  • Hydration
  • Salt water gargles
  • Avoid aspirin

GAS Pharyngitis

  • Penicillin V
  • Amoxicillin
  • Benzathine penicillin G
  • Cephalexin / Clindamycin / Macrolides for allergy
  • Complete full 10-day course

Controversies, Recent Updates, and Landmark Trials

  • Testing in low-risk settings debated
  • Short-course antibiotics under study
  • Tonsillectomy criteria refined
  • GAS vaccine development ongoing
  • Expansion of molecular diagnostics
