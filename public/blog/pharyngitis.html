Throat Infections in Children

Understanding Pharyngitis: Causes, Symptoms, Diagnosis, and Management

Definition and Epidemiology

Throat infections, also known as pharyngitis or tonsillopharyngitis, are acute inflammations of the pharynx and/or tonsils. In children, these are primarily viral, accounting for 70-85% of cases. The most common bacterial cause is Group A Streptococcus (GAS), specifically *Streptococcus pyogenes*, responsible for 15-30% of cases in children and adolescents, with a peak incidence between 5 and 15 years of age. Less common bacterial agents include *Arcanobacterium haemolyticum*, *Mycoplasma pneumoniae*, and *Chlamydophila pneumoniae*. Fungal infections are rare and typically affect immunocompromised individuals. Non-infectious causes like allergies, irritants, or gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) can mimic these symptoms [1, 2, 3].

Pathophysiology

The underlying mechanism involves pathogen entry and replication in the pharyngeal mucosa, triggering a localized inflammatory response.

Viral Pharyngitis

  • Viruses such as rhinovirus, adenovirus, influenza virus, parainfluenza virus, enterovirus, respiratory syncytial virus, and Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infect pharyngeal epithelial cells.
  • Viral replication leads to cell damage and an inflammatory cascade involving cytokine release, causing vasodilation, edema, and pain.
  • EBV can cause infectious mononucleosis, characterized by significant lymphoid hyperplasia in the tonsils and cervical lymph nodes [4, 5].

Bacterial Pharyngitis (Group A Streptococcus)

  • *Streptococcus pyogenes* attaches to host epithelial cells via surface proteins like M protein, lipoteichoic acid, and F protein.
  • It produces virulence factors including streptolysins O and S (cell lysis), pyrogenic exotoxins (superantigens causing scarlet fever rash), and streptokinase (fibrinolysis).
  • These factors help evade host defenses, cause local tissue damage and inflammation, leading to exudative pharyngitis.
  • The M protein also prevents phagocytosis and is linked to post-streptococcal sequelae such as acute rheumatic fever and post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis through molecular mimicry [6, 7].

Clinical Presentation

Symptoms vary by etiology, with considerable overlap between viral and bacterial infections.

General Symptoms

Include sore throat (odynophagia), difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), fever, headache, and malaise.

Viral Pharyngitis

Often accompanied by coryza (rhinorrhea, nasal congestion), cough, hoarseness, conjunctivitis, oral ulcers (e.g., herpangina from coxsackievirus), or diarrhea. The pharynx may appear red, with or without exudates. Adenovirus can cause pharyngoconjunctival fever [4, 8].

Bacterial Pharyngitis (Group A Streptococcus)

Classic Presentation

Abrupt onset of sore throat, painful swallowing, fever, headache, nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain (particularly in younger children).

Physical Examination

Erythematous pharynx, often with tonsillar exudates (white/yellow patches), palatal petechiae, tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, and absence of cough, rhinorrhea, or conjunctivitis.

Scarlet Fever

May co-occur with GAS pharyngitis, presenting as a fine, sandpaper-like, blanching erythematous rash, typically starting on the neck and chest and spreading. Desquamation occurs during recovery. A "strawberry tongue" (initially white-coated with red papillae, later uniformly red) is also characteristic [1, 9].

Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)

A severe complication characterized by severe unilateral sore throat, fever, trismus, a "hot potato" voice, drooling, and uvular deviation [10].

Diagnostic Criteria

The goal of diagnosis is to distinguish GAS pharyngitis from viral causes to avoid unnecessary antibiotics while ensuring timely treatment for GAS to prevent complications.

1. Clinical Assessment

Centor/Modified McIsaac Score: These clinical prediction rules estimate the probability of GAS pharyngitis and guide testing decisions, especially in patients aged 3 years and older.

Modified McIsaac Score components (+1 point each):

  • Tonsillar exudates
  • Swollen/tender anterior cervical lymph nodes
  • History of fever (>100.4°F or 38°C)
  • Absence of cough
  • Age 3-14 years (additional point)
  • Age >44 years (subtract a point)

Interpretation (IDSA 2012 guidelines):

  • Score 0-1: Low GAS probability; typically no testing or antibiotics.
  • Score 2-3: Intermediate GAS probability; consider rapid antigen detection test (RADT).
  • Score ≥4: High GAS probability; consider RADT or empiric antibiotics [1].

2. Laboratory Confirmation for GAS

Rapid Antigen Detection Tests (RADT)

Highly specific (95-99%) but with variable sensitivity (70-90%). A positive RADT confirms GAS and warrants antibiotic initiation. A negative RADT in children and adolescents often requires a confirmatory throat culture due to lower sensitivity, particularly in areas with high acute rheumatic fever prevalence. In adults, a negative RADT typically rules out GAS [1, 13].

Throat Culture

The gold standard for GAS diagnosis, with nearly 100% sensitivity and specificity for symptomatic individuals. Results take 24-48 hours. A throat culture is recommended if the RADT is negative in children and adolescents to confirm the absence of GAS [1, 13].

Molecular Assays (NAATs)

Nucleic acid amplification tests for GAS are highly sensitive and specific with rapid turnaround times (minutes to hours). They are increasingly used as a single diagnostic test for GAS, potentially eliminating the need for confirmatory cultures [13, 14].

Management

Management depends on the etiology: symptomatic treatment for viral pharyngitis and antibiotic therapy for GAS pharyngitis.

A. Viral Pharyngitis

Symptomatic Treatment:

  • Analgesia and Antipyresis: Acetaminophen (10-15 mg/kg orally every 4-6 hours, max 75 mg/kg/day) or ibuprofen (5-10 mg/kg orally every 6-8 hours, max 40 mg/kg/day for children >6 months) for pain and fever relief [15].
  • Hydration: Encourage oral fluid intake to prevent dehydration.
  • Comfort Measures: Warm salt water gargles (for older children), lozenges, or throat sprays for temporary relief [4].
  • Avoid Aspirin: Due to the risk of Reye's syndrome in children with viral illnesses [16].

B. Group A Streptococcus Pharyngitis

Antibiotic treatment is indicated for confirmed GAS pharyngitis to prevent acute rheumatic fever, reduce suppurative complications (e.g., peritonsillar abscess), and shorten symptom duration and infectivity [1].

First-Line Treatment (IDSA 2012, NICE 2018):

  • Penicillin V: Preferred due to narrow spectrum, efficacy, safety, and low cost. GAS remains universally susceptible to penicillin [1, 17].
    • Dosage: 250 mg orally 2-3 times daily for children <27 kg OR 500 mg orally 2-3 times daily for children ≥27 kg, for 10 days [1].
  • Amoxicillin: Equally effective as penicillin V, often preferred for children due to better palatability and once or twice-daily dosing options, improving adherence [1, 18].
    • Dosage: 50 mg/kg orally once daily (max 1000 mg/day) OR 25 mg/kg orally twice daily (max 500 mg/dose), for 10 days [1].
  • Benzathine Penicillin G (intramuscular): For patients with poor adherence or social barriers to oral therapy. Single-dose treatment.
    • Dosage: 600,000 units IM for children <27 kg OR 1,200,000 units IM for children ≥27 kg [1]. (Class I, Level A)

Penicillin Allergy:

  • Non-anaphylactic/delayed-type allergy:
    • Cephalexin: 20 mg/kg orally twice daily (max 500 mg/dose), for 10 days [1].
    • Cefadroxil: 30 mg/kg orally once daily (max 1000 mg/day), for 10 days [1].
  • Anaphylactic/immediate-type allergy:
    • Clindamycin: 7 mg/kg orally 3 times daily (max 300 mg/dose), for 10 days [1].
    • Azithromycin: 12 mg/kg orally once daily (max 500 mg/day), for 5 days [1]. (Shorter duration is acceptable, but resistance is a concern; close monitoring for treatment failure is advised) [1, 19].
    • Clarithromycin: 7.5 mg/kg orally twice daily (max 250 mg/dose), for 10 days [1].
    • (Note on Macrolides: Increasing macrolide resistance in GAS is observed, especially with erythromycin. Susceptibility testing may be warranted for treatment failure or high local resistance rates) [1, 19].

Duration of Therapy:

10 days for oral penicillin V, amoxicillin, cephalexin, cefadroxil, clindamycin, and clarithromycin. Azithromycin is for 5 days. Completing the full course is crucial to prevent rheumatic fever, even if symptoms resolve earlier [1].

Treatment Failure:

Persistent GAS pharyngitis or recurrent infections may require re-evaluation of adherence, consideration of other pathogens, or antibiotic change. Clindamycin or amoxicillin-clavulanate might be considered, though evidence for superiority is not robust [1].

Carrier State:

Up to 20% of children can be asymptomatic GAS carriers. They generally do not require antibiotic treatment unless there's an active outbreak of rheumatic fever, recurrent GAS infections within a household, or a personal history of rheumatic fever [1, 20].

Controversies, Recent Updates, and Landmark Trials

1. Diagnosis in Low-Risk Settings

The necessity of routine GAS testing in areas with low acute rheumatic fever incidence is debated. Some guidelines suggest clinical judgment alone might suffice in very low-risk children in such settings, especially with limited resources [21]. However, IDSA strongly recommends testing [1].

2. Short-Course Antibiotic Therapy

While a 10-day course is standard for most oral antibiotics to prevent rheumatic fever, shorter durations are explored. A 5-day azithromycin course is acceptable but faces increasing resistance. A 5-day cephalosporin course has shown comparable efficacy to 10-day penicillin V in some studies, but general guidelines still recommend 10 days for beta-lactams due to robust evidence for rheumatic fever prevention [1, 22].

3. Role of Tonsillectomy

Indications for tonsillectomy for recurrent pharyngitis are refined. Current guidelines (AAO-HNS 2019) recommend it for specific criteria: ≥7 episodes in the preceding year, ≥5 episodes/year for 2 years, or ≥3 episodes/year for 3 years, provided each episode is well-documented and debilitating [23]. Shared decision-making is emphasized.

4. Vaccine Development

Research is ongoing for a GAS vaccine to prevent infections and sequelae. Challenges include M protein diversity and the need for broad protection. Several vaccine candidates are in development [24].

5. Molecular Diagnostics

The increasing availability of highly sensitive and specific NAATs for GAS allows for rapid and accurate diagnosis, potentially streamlining management by reducing the need for confirmatory cultures after RADT and enabling earlier therapy [13, 14].
